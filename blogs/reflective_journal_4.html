Reflective Journal 4

Month 10 session - Patients, Providers, Payers, and Policy Makers

During this month's session on Patients, Providers, Payers, and Policy Makers, I was particularly struck by how practical and outcome‑oriented stakeholder analysis can be. The emphasis on understanding the value we are expected to deliver, and then engagement with users, decision‑makers and policy makers, felt very relevant to openEHR work for the clinical setting. Ideas such as asking stakeholders “What data do you want to see?” and keeping the underlying data layer hidden, while focusing on the information and outcomes they care about were very interesting. Ensuring broad representation and good governance across all stakeholder groups also resonated with me.

The brief introduction to Zotero (https://www.zotero.org/) also gave me useful inspiration beyond formal research. Even though I am not currently doing extensive academic work, I can see clear value in using Zotero—or a similar tool—to organise documents, references and shared knowledge around openEHR, interoperability and digital strategy.

Month 11 session - Patients, Providers, Payers, and Policy Makers

This final fellowship session strengthened my understanding of federation and research uses of health data, especially using openEHR. Across the talks, I saw how openEHR-based modelling, interoperability standards, and well-designed infrastructure can enable practical applications such as infection control, large research cohorts, and multi-site collaboration.

Pascal Biermann's presentation on the Smart Infection Control System (SmiCS) from HiGHmed stood out to me, especially the way openEHR templates were turned into a usable tool for clinicians. The network graph visualizations of infection contacts and chains was right up my alley. I also learned about SUS/cSUS usability scales, which I hadn't known before, and realized this could have been useful for evaluating my own fellowship project.

Cecilia Mascia and Giovanni Delussu's talk on BBMRI-ERIC and the colorectal cancer cohort gave a detailed, realistic view of research data harmonisation. I enjoyed the clear walkthrough of the conversion workflow (from an existing database through transformations into openEHR compositions) and the challenges they highlighted, especially dealing with variable input data quality, which closely mirrored issues we face in my workgroup.

Reto Wettstein's overview of the Data Sharing Framework (DSF) in the German Medical Informatics Initiative was a highlight for understanding how federated research can work in practice. The technical explanation of the peer-to-peer setup, security approach, and DSF node design helped me see how feasibility and data-sharing processes can be coordinated across hospitals.

Month 12 - That's all for now

Looking back, the Archetype Companion has been my testing ground for openEHR workflow ideas - a mix of strong and weaker concepts that now form a solid base for future refinement. The Fellowship itself proved to be an excellent framework to engage more deeply with the openEHR community and to show who I am and how I work, opening the door to future collaborations.

One year turned out to be an acceptable - if very fast - timespan, which made a sharp problem statement and upfront planning absolutely essential to keep the project on track. I feel genuinely fortunate to have been given the freedom to explore creative ideas and user‑centred workflows, while at the same time building substantial technical skills in JavaScript, GitHub automation, regex-based parsing, and general software delivery practices.

Overall, the Fellowship has been a very positive experience for me, made possible by the exceptionally strong support of my superiors and the openEHR community. I would like to thank my superiors at CatSalut, Jordi Piera and Lluis Valle, for the time and trust they invested in this work. My special thanks go to Heather Leslie, whose initial idea and ongoing guidance were the cornerstone of this project. Finally, I am deeply grateful to the openEHR Fellowship programme and everyone involved in its organisation and management; without their effort, this project would not have been possible.
