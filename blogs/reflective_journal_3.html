Fellowship session Month 7 - The Standards Landscape

I'm very happy to have participated in the session for month 7. I found both presentations extremely informative and useful.

The presentation by John Meredith (EY) was especially interesting regarding the pitfalls of interoperability and the benefits of an open system approach. Although the talk was quite rapid-fire and some things went over my head, the main ideas were clear to me. One key takeaway is that semantics are really important - interoperability always comes at a cost, because every data transformation destroys a part of the semantic meaning. The further we are in the development process, the more expensive it becomes to change the data pipeline. It's clear that everyone has to conform and standardize, but standards alone aren't enough. We need open platforms to actually operationalize open standards, and that's where FHIR and openEHR come in. The aim is to have a semantically coherent, canonical foundation, where the open nature of the platform enables a shared modeling effort.

The presentation by Luis Marco Ruiz (vitagroup) was very practical, which I appreciated. He gave a detailed description of the data pathway, starting from patient consent on paper, moving to implementation in FHIR, including openEHR clinical data, and finally transmitting everything to a central repository for research. I found the discussion about the impact of German law on anonymization particularly interesting, as it showed how the legal framework dictates the technical solution-in this case, requiring double pseudonymization of the data.

Overall, I found both presentations valuable and thought-provoking.

Fellowship session Month 8 - Real World Cases

The Month 8 session with Luke Readman on One London showed me how hard it is to truly connect health and care systems, even within a single city. London is officially one region, but in practice it is a complex web of programmes, organisations and technologies that all need to work together. The London Care Record, the Universal Care Plan and the wider One London efforts illustrated how far reality is from the clean, integrated vision we often talk about.

The “Titanic strategy” warning really stayed with me: it is tempting to focus on the shiny, visible innovations above the surface while neglecting the large, invisible foundations underneath. Repeated reinvention and persistent inequities are, in a way, symptoms of this imbalance. For me, this connects directly to my openEHR work and clinical data repositories: advanced apps and analytics only make sense if they rest on a solid, resilient data and governance layer.

Leadership stood out as the key principle. Not as vague inspiration, but as the ability to define a clear shared strategy, insist on standards, keep equity in view, and resist being distracted by short-term wins. The One London Citizens' Summit also showed that leadership here includes engaging the public and building trust, not just managing technology.

This leads to a question I am still asking myself: how can we introduce innovation into such a complicated network without draining resources from building and maintaining the foundational infrastructure? In my own context, I feel a strong pull towards new tools and features, but this session reminded me that real progress depends on disciplined focus: investing first in interoperability, governance and robust data structures, and making sure that any innovation strengthens, rather than undermines, that base.
