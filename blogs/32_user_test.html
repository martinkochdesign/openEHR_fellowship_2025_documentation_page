Summary of Volunteer Responses: Archetype Companion User Testing

The first round of user testing for the Archetype Companion has attracted a diverse group of eight volunteers, representing a range of professional roles including clinical modellers, data modellers, medical information specialists, advisers, and interoperability coordinators. The cohort's experience with openEHR varies from newcomers with less than two years of experience to seasoned professionals with over six years in the field.

The projects proposed for testing reflect a broad spectrum of clinical and technical interests. Several participants are focused on developing or refining templates for specific clinical scenarios, such as patient admission, rounds, discharge, and adverse reactions. Others are interested in applying the Archetype Companion to national projects, risk factor assessments, and specialized therapies like instillation therapy for bladder cancer and intravitreal injection therapy. Some volunteers are exploring foundational use cases in areas like imaging, medication, transfusions, critical care, and alerts or notifications.

Overall, the cohort brings together both early-career and experienced professionals, ensuring a wide range of perspectives and use cases. Their collective input will provide valuable feedback on the Archetype Companion's usability and applicability across different clinical domains and project stages.

Archetype Companion – Soft Launch Feedback Analysis

Participants: 8 invited · 4 questionnaire responses (anonymous) · Period: 09–30 Dec 2025

1. Overall Sentiment and Key Strengths

1.1 Overall Experience

Using a 4-point scale (Poor=1, Fair=2, Good=3, Very good=4), average overall rating: (3 + 4 + 3 + 3) / 4 = 3.25 → between Good and Very good.

1.2 What Users Liked

a) Search & navigation of archetypes
“The visualization of the relationship of archetypes, and the information about archetypes (such as RM information) and the search is well done and easy.”
“Very useful for browsing & searching archetypes… especially pointing out different connections/inclusions between Archetypes.”
b) Network-like visualization & relationships
“I like the searches that can be made using the tool, using different combination and watching the relationships.”
“Visualization surprisingly responsive and appealing… Navigating the archetype dependencies in the graph was very easy.”
c) Archetype information & RM context
“I like how the information of archetypes is shown in the layout and easier to understand, comparing with CKM, for example: more intuitive, and better explained and shown.”
“Support for checklists and a smooth way to explore specifications and the Reference Model.”
d) Overall UX for clinical modellers
“Among all the tools in the openEHR ecosystem, this is the one I’ve found to be most thoughtfully designed for clinical modellers… With the addition of a few targeted features, it has the potential to become the most valuable and widely used tool in our workflow.”
e) New archetype placeholder / template planning concept
“To be able to keep slots for archetypes not yet created in the template design mode… super useful. It hasn’t been yet embraced in other tools.”
f) Exporter
“The exporter is also very useful.”
g) Tutorials and explanations
“I think tutorials and explanations are excellent.”

Tutorials are clearly noticed and used; however, some videos fail to load (see issues).

Conclusion: The core concept and overall direction are strongly validated. Users see the Archetype Companion as aligned with clinical modelling workflows and solving real problems, especially around exploration and understanding of archetypes and their relationships.

2. Main Pain Points and Issues

2.1 Usability & Interaction Problems

a) Template Planner - element editing friction
  • Users must manually add each required element from an archetype, one by one, and type each element name.
  • The element name field in the Template Planner does not allow text selection for deletion or replacement.
“Having to manually add every required element from an archetype, one by one, and having to manually type each element name… more time-consuming and increases the risk of inconsistencies.”
“In Template Planner, text fields are not selectable for deletion. I have to keep DELETE pressed to delete a string.”
b) Difficulty understanding UI layouts and flows
  • Users find it hard to get used to the different layouts and to work with the tool in a “deeper way” when designing.
  • Specifically unclear: how to add archetypes to a collection and how to view which archetypes are currently in the collection.
“There are different layouts and, even if I have been trying the tool for the last week, I am still getting familiar with them and its functionalities.”
“I found it difficult to see how to add the archetypes to my collection, or to check which archetypes are added to my collection.”
c) Browser-specific UI issues (Firefox)
  • In Firefox (145.0.2), dropdown menus appear like browser “Autofill” instead of real dropdowns.
  • Item labels are truncated with “…” and often unreadable.
“Dropdown menus are not displayed well: Displayed like ‘Autofill’ suggestions instead of real dropdowns, and item labels are truncated with ‘...’, making most of them not readable.”
d) Tutorial video reliability
  • Tutorial videos 6 and 9 failed to load for at least one user.
“Tutorial videos 6 and 9 were not loading for me.”
e) Graph navigation ergonomics
  • History navigation is powerful but not obvious; discovered through tutorials.
  • Users want a simple way to go “up one step” in the dependency graph directly from the visualization.
“I discovered in the tutorial video that I can go back via the ‘History’. However, it would be useful to have a button in the visualization for going ‘upwards’ one step instead of only being able to go downwards on the dependency tree.”

2.2 Missing or Requested Features

a) Template Planner – structure loading & editing
  • Automatically assign A/E typology to archetype elements.
  • Load the inherent structure of the published archetype into the Template Planner (instead of building it manually).
  • Option to load original element names from the archetype by default.
  • Add the ability to hide elements (set occurrences to 0..0) without deleting them.
  • Support value sets / terminology bindings for ELEMENTs of type CODED_TEXT.
“It would be valuable to automatically assign the A/E typology to archetype elements, or to load the inherent structure of the published archetype.”
“Adding the ability to specify value sets or terminology bindings for ELEMENTS of type CODED TEXT would greatly improve modelling precision and consistency.”
b) Export and integration with other tools
  • Ability to upload the designed template to an archetype designer / another design tool.
  • Download templates in OPT or OET format, or another suitable format.
“It would be useful, in the future, to find somehow to be able to upload the template designed in this tool, to the archetype designer… Or to be able to download the template designed in another format.”
“Finally, a download option for templates in OPT or OET format would be useful (although I understand this might not be the purpose of the tool).”
c) Additional visualization modes
  • Mind map view for archetypes and template designs.
“Another thing that would be helpful is to have a visualization of the archetypes and templates design in a mind map view.”
d) Archetype ID support
  • Automatic generation of archetype IDs from concept name and class according to openEHR conventions.
“Archetype IDs follow a specific structure. The companion could automatically generate this ID from the ‘Concept name’ and ‘Class’ fields instead of having to write it myself.”

3. Thematic Improvement Areas

3.1 Template Planner Workflow

Problems:

  • Manual, repetitive and error-prone element creation.
  • Poor text editing (no selection/replacement) in element fields.
  • No way to simply hide (0..0) elements; only deletion.
  • No support for value sets / terminology bindings for CODED_TEXT.

Why it matters: Template Planner is key for turning exploration into actual modelling work. Current friction risks limiting the tool to a browse-only prototype instead of an everyday tool.

3.2 UI Learnability & Discoverability

Problems:

  • Multiple layouts and views are confusing for new users.
  • Critical workflows (e.g., adding archetypes to a collection and viewing the collection) are not obvious.

Desired: Clearer labels, affordances and guidance; more explicit “Add to collection” and “My collection” areas, and potentially lightweight onboarding for first-time users.

3.3 Robustness & Cross-Browser Polish

Problems:

  • Firefox rendering issues for dropdowns, making options unreadable.
  • Some tutorial videos do not load, reducing self-service learning.

3.4 Graph Navigation Enhancements

Problem: Users can navigate down dependency trees easily but lack an obvious “up one step” control, and the powerful History feature is not visible enough.

3.5 Export & Ecosystem Integration

Problem: Templates created or planned in the Companion are not yet easily usable in downstream tools (archetype designers, runtime environments).

3.6 Additional Visualization Mode

Opportunity: A mind map view could provide a complementary, more conceptual overview of archetypes and templates, especially useful in early-stage planning and stakeholder discussions.

3.7 Archetype ID Assistance

Opportunity: Auto-generating archetype IDs from concept name and class can reduce errors and speed up archetype creation.

4. Prioritized Improvements and Importance

Priorities are grouped into three levels:

  • P1 – Critical Directly affects core workflow or basic usability.
  • P2 – High Strongly improves productivity/adoption; not strictly blocking.
  • P3 – Nice-to-have Valuable but can be scheduled after P1/P2 items.
  • P4 – Out of scope Feature out of scope that might be of interest when the scope is re-defined.
Priority Improvement Rationale / Impact
P1 Fix Template Planner text fields (selection & editing) Users cannot select text to delete or replace; must hold DELETE. This affects every edit in the Planner and creates strong frustration in a core workflow.
P1 Fix Firefox dropdown rendering & truncation Dropdowns render like browser autofill, labels are truncated and unreadable. Firefox users cannot reliably access many configuration options; this undermines perceived quality and basic usability.
P1 Ensure all tutorial videos load reliably (esp. 6 & 9) Tutorials are central to learning a multi-layout UI. Broken videos reduce learnability and directly contradict the otherwise positive impression of the tutorials.
P2 Auto-load archetype structure in Template Planner Instead of manually adding every element, the Planner should import the published archetype structure and original element names. This turns the tool from a prototype into a practical daily tool for modellers.
P2 Enhance graph navigation with “up one level” control Adds a clear navigation affordance directly in the graph view so users can go up the dependency tree without relying only on the History list.
P2 Auto-generate archetype IDs from concept name & class Reduces cognitive load and mistakes with openEHR ID conventions; speeds up creation and encourages consistency across artefacts.
P3 Clarify & streamline “collection” workflow It is unclear how to add archetypes to a collection and how to view the collection. This blocks a key promise of the tool: moving from search to a curated set for template design.
P3 Add “hide element” (0..0) in Template Planner Allowing users to hide elements (set occurrences 0..0) instead of deleting them matches how template specialisation typically works, and reduces the risk of losing information.
P3 Add mind map view for archetypes/templates Provides an additional high-level visualisation useful in early design and communication, especially with stakeholders less familiar with technical dependency graphs.
P3 Additional onboarding aids for multi-layout UI Once core usability issues are fixed, add guided tours, first-time tooltips, or a “design your first template in 5 steps” overlay to further reduce learning curve.
P4 Support value sets / terminology bindings for CODED_TEXT Being able to define value sets and terminology bindings is essential for serious data modelling.
P4 Export templates in OPT/OET and/or other interoperable formats Makes the Archetype Companion part of the real modelling pipeline instead of a standalone exploration tool, enabling users to continue work in their usual archetype/template designers.

5. Strategic Summary

  • Validated strengths: network visualisation, archetype search, RM integration, clear archetype information, and the concept of new-archetype placeholders are all strongly appreciated.
  • Main adoption blockers: friction in the Template Planner, confusing collection flow, Firefox visual bugs, and unreliable tutorial videos.
  • High-leverage direction: focus on “from browsing to production templates”:
    • Search and visualise → add to collection → auto-load archetypes into Template Planner → tweak via hide/constraints & terminology bindings → export (OPT/OET) or send to archetype designer.
